### Section 1: Before You Start (Reality Check)
**~2 pages**

**Hook:** "Let's have an honest conversation before you quit your day job."

**Content:**
- The 20% year-one failure statistic (BLS 2024)
- The #1 reason contractors fail: cash flow (82% - U.S. Bank)
- Construction is the 2nd most likely industry to fail (after Information)
- But also: 80% survive their first year. You can be one of them.

**Quiz: Are You Ready?**
- [ ] I have 6+ months of personal expenses saved
- [ ] I have my trade skills down (journeyman level or equivalent experience)
- [ ] I know what I'll charge (or have a plan to figure it out)
- [ ] I have 3-5 people who would hire me today
- [ ] I'm okay with uncertainty and irregular income
- [ ] I have a fallback plan if this doesn't work

**Scoring:**
- 5-6 checked: You're in a strong position
- 3-4 checked: Proceed with caution, address the gaps
- 0-2 checked: Build your foundation first

**Side Hustle vs. Cold Start Decision:**
- Side hustle path: Keep your job, build customers on weekends/evenings, transition when revenue is consistent (12-24 months typical)
- Cold start path: Jump in full-time with runway saved (need 6-12 months expenses minimum)
- The "anchor client" strategy: One client providing 50%+ of income reduces risk

### Section 2: Legal Setup Checklist
**~2 pages**

**Opening:** "Get the paperwork right from day one. It's not as hard as you think."

**The Checklist:**

**Business Structure (Do this first):**
- [ ] **Sole Proprietorship** - Simplest, no paperwork beyond business license, BUT no liability protection
- [ ] **LLC** - Recommended for most contractors. Protects personal assets. $50-500 to form depending on state.
- [ ] **S-Corp** - Consider once you're making $75K+ profit (tax advantages)

*Recommendation: Start as LLC. It's worth the $100-300 filing fee for the protection.*

**Federal Requirements:**
- [ ] **EIN (Employer Identification Number)** - Free from IRS.gov, takes 5 minutes online
- [ ] Use EIN instead of SSN on contracts and W-9s

**State Requirements:**

| Your State | General Contractor License Required? |
|------------|-------------------------------------|
| Alabama | Yes, projects >$50K (>$10K residential) |
| Alaska | Yes, all work |
| Arizona | Yes, all work |
| Arkansas | Yes, commercial >$50K, residential >$2K |
| California | Yes, projects >$500 |
| Colorado | No statewide (check city/county) |
| ... | [Full table in Appendix] |

**33 states require state contractor licenses. 17 do not.** Check the appendix for your state.

- [ ] **State contractor license** (if required)
- [ ] **Local business license** (almost always required - check your city/county)
- [ ] **Trade-specific license** (electricians, plumbers, HVAC - usually required regardless of state contractor license)

**Surety Bond (if required):**
- Bonds range from $5,000-$100,000 depending on state
- Cost: 1-5% of bond amount annually (good credit = lower %)
- Example: $15,000 bond = $150-$450/year

**Trade-Specific Licenses:**

| Painter | Usually none beyond business license |
| General Contractor | State license if required + local permits |

### Section 3: Insurance Checklist
**~1.5 pages**

**Opening:** "Insurance isn't optional. It protects your business AND your personal assets."

**The Checklist:**

**Required (for most contractors):**
- [ ] **General Liability Insurance** - $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate minimum
  - Typical cost: $82-150/month
  - Protects against property damage, bodily injury claims
  - Most clients require this before you start work

**Required (if you have employees):**
- [ ] **Workers Compensation** - Required in most states once you have employees
  - Typical cost: $142-254/month
  - California requires WC even for solo contractors in certain trades (roofing, HVAC, concrete)
  - Check your state requirements

**Required (if using vehicle for work):**
- [ ] **Commercial Auto Insurance** - Personal auto policies don't cover business use
  - Typical cost: $80-200/month
  - Covers accidents while driving to/from jobs

**Recommended:**
- [ ] **Inland Marine / Tools Coverage** - Covers your tools and equipment
  - Typical cost: $25-75/month
  - Worth it if you have $10K+ in tools

**Cost Estimates by Trade:**
| Trade | GL Only | GL + WC (solo) | GL + WC + Auto |
|-------|---------|----------------|----------------|
| Electrician | $100-150/mo | $200-300/mo | $300-450/mo |
| Plumber | $100-140/mo | $200-300/mo | $280-400/mo |
| HVAC | $120-180/mo | $220-350/mo | $320-500/mo |
| Painter | $60-100/mo | $150-250/mo | $230-400/mo |
| GC | $85-150/mo | $180-320/mo | $280-450/mo |

*Sources: MoneyGeek, Insureon, Progressive Commercial (2025-2026)*

**What to Tell the Insurance Agent:**
1. Your trade and typical job types
2. Annual revenue estimate (even if it's $0 to start)
3. Whether you have employees or will have them
4. What equipment/tools you own
5. Service area (some states cost more)

**Pro tip:** Get quotes from 3+ insurers. Prices vary wildly.

### Section 4: Money Setup
**~1.5 pages**

**Opening:** "Cash flow kills more contractors than lack of work. Get this right."

**The 82% Statistic:**
According to a U.S. Bank study, 82% of business failures cite cash flow problems. In construction, the average Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) is 83 days. That means you wait nearly 3 months for payment while still paying for materials, gas, and bills.

**The Checklist:**

**Bank Accounts (Do this immediately):**
- [ ] **Business checking account** - Keep business money separate from personal. Period.
- [ ] **Business savings account** - For taxes and emergency fund

*Why this matters: Mixing personal and business money is the #1 accounting mistake. It makes taxes a nightmare and looks bad if you're ever audited.*

**Accounting System:**
- [ ] Choose one: QuickBooks, Wave (free), or even a spreadsheet
- [ ] Track every expense from day one
- [ ] Set aside 25-30% of every payment for taxes

**Emergency Fund Target:**
- 3 months operating expenses: Minimum
- 6 months operating expenses: Recommended
- 12 months: Conservative (smart if no existing clients)

**Payment Terms You'll Use:**
- **Small jobs (<$2,000):** Due on completion
- **Medium jobs ($2,000-10,000):** 50% deposit, 50% on completion
- **Large jobs (>$10,000):** 30-50% deposit, progress payments, 10% on final walkthrough

*Note: Some states cap deposit amounts. California limits deposits to $1,000 or 10% for home improvement contracts.*

**How You'll Accept Payments:**
- [ ] **Check** - Still common, but slow (deposits take 1-2 days)
- [ ] **ACH/Bank Transfer** - Low fees (~0.8%), fast
- [ ] **Credit Card** - 2.6-2.9% fees, but faster payment from clients
- [ ] **Cash** - For small jobs, but track it for taxes

**The Cash Flow Mindset:**
- Invoice immediately after completing work (same day)
- Follow up on overdue invoices within 3 days
- Don't start new jobs if old invoices are 60+ days unpaid
- Build payment milestones into larger contracts

### Section 6: Your First 10 Customers
**~1.5 pages**

**Opening:** "You don't need a marketing budget. You need 10 people who trust you."

**The Friends & Family Launch:**
Your first 5-10 jobs will likely come from people who already know you. That's not cheating - that's smart business.

- Make a list of everyone you know who owns a home or business
- Tell them you're starting your business
- Ask: "Do you know anyone who needs [your trade] work?"
- Offer a "friends and family" rate for your first few jobs (but don't work for free)

**The Neighborhood Effect:**
One of the best marketing strategies costs nothing: do great work, and neighbors notice.

- When you're working on a job, other homeowners see your truck
- Hand out business cards to anyone who walks by
- Ask your customer if they'd recommend you to neighbors
- "While you're here" jobs from neighbors can double your revenue on a single visit

**Pricing Your First Jobs:**
The biggest mistake new contractors make: underpricing to "get the work."

- Don't charge less than you would as an employee (your time isn't worth less now)
- Use the pricing guide or calculate: Desired salary ÷ billable hours + overhead + profit margin
- If you feel uncomfortable quoting, you're probably in the right range

**Building Reputation from Day One:**
- [ ] Take before/after photos of every job
- [ ] Ask for Google reviews (87% of people read reviews before hiring a contractor)
- [ ] Create a simple Google Business Profile (free)
- [ ] Respond to every review, even negative ones

**How to Ask for Referrals:**
After completing a job, say:
> "I'm just getting my business started and I'm looking for more customers like you. If you know anyone who needs [electrical/plumbing/etc.] work, I'd really appreciate it if you'd pass along my number."

Then follow up in 2-3 weeks to stay top of mind.

### Section 9: Common First-Year Mistakes
**~1 page**

**Opening:** "Learn from others' failures so you don't repeat them."

**Mistake #1: Underpricing to Win Jobs**
The trap: "I'll charge less to get the work, then raise prices later."
The reality: Cheap clients expect cheap forever. Racing to the bottom kills your business.
The fix: Calculate your true costs, add profit margin, and charge what you're worth.

*CTA: "Learn exactly how to price jobs profitably → The Contractor Pricing Guide ($29) at quotecat.ai/pricing-guide"*

**Mistake #2: No Contracts or Written Quotes**
The trap: "We agreed on the phone, that's good enough."
The reality: Verbal agreements lead to disputes. "That's not what I said" is expensive.
The fix: Everything in writing. Even for small jobs. Even for friends.

**Mistake #3: Mixing Personal and Business Money**
The trap: "I'll sort it out at tax time."
The reality: You won't remember what was business vs. personal 8 months later.
The fix: Separate accounts from day one. Use your business card/account for all business expenses.

**Mistake #4: Taking On Too Much Too Fast**
The trap: "I can't say no to work!"
The reality: 40% of contractor failures are caused by overextension (Travelers Insurance).
The fix: It's okay to say "I'm booked out 3 weeks." Better to do fewer jobs well than many jobs poorly.

**Mistake #5: Not Tracking Time and Costs**
The trap: "I know how long things take."
The reality: You're probably wrong. And you're probably undercharging.
The fix: Track your hours and material costs on every job for at least your first 20 jobs. Adjust pricing based on real data.
