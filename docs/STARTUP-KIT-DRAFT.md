## Side Hustle vs. Cold Start

There are two paths to starting your contracting business:

### The Side Hustle Path

Keep your day job. Build customers on weekends and evenings. Transition to full-time when revenue is consistent.

**Timeline:** 12-24 months (typical)

**Pros:**
- Lower financial risk
- Time to learn the business side while still getting a paycheck
- Build customer base before you need it

**Cons:**
- Slower growth
- Less availability = fewer job opportunities
- Exhausting to work two jobs

**Best for:** People with limited savings, those who need health insurance, first-time business owners.

### The Cold Start Path

Quit your job. Go all-in from day one with runway saved.

**Timeline:** Consider having 6-12 months of personal expenses saved (minimum)

**Pros:**
- Full focus on building the business
- More availability = more job opportunities
- Faster growth potential

**Cons:**
- Higher financial risk
- Stress of no steady income
- No safety net if it doesn't work

**Best for:** People with more savings, an existing customer base, or a working spouse/partner.

### The Anchor Client Strategy

The smartest approach: find one client who can provide 50%+ of your income before you quit.

This might be:
- A general contractor who needs a reliable sub
- A property manager with regular maintenance needs
- A business owner who needs ongoing work

An anchor client gives you some income stability AND time to build the rest of your business.

# Section 2: Legal Setup Checklist

Get the paperwork right from day one. It's not as hard as you think.

## Business Structure (Do This First)

You have three main options:

### Sole Proprietorship
- **Simplest option** - No special paperwork beyond a business license
- **Cost:** $0-$50 depending on local requirements
- **Consideration:** No separation between you and your business legally, an LLC provides more protection

### LLC (Limited Liability Company)
- **Recommended for most contractors**
- **Cost:** $50-$500 depending on state (one-time filing fee)
- **Benefit:** Protects your personal assets (house, car, savings) from business liabilities
- **Tax treatment:** "Pass-through" - business income goes on your personal taxes

### S-Corporation
- **Consider once you're making $75K+ profit**
- **Cost:** More expensive to set up and maintain ($500-$2,000+ annually for accounting)
- **Benefit:** Potential tax savings through salary/dividend split
- **Downside:** More paperwork, need to pay yourself a "reasonable salary"

**Recommendation:** Start as an LLC. The $100-$300 filing fee is worth it for the protection. You can always convert to S-Corp later when you're making more money.

*Consult an accountant before choosing your structure, they can advise based on your specific situation.*

---

## Federal Requirements

### EIN (Employer Identification Number)
- **What it is:** A tax ID number for your business (like a Social Security number)
- **Cost:** Free
- **How to get it:** IRS.gov → Apply Online → Takes 5 minutes
- **Why you need it:** Required for business bank accounts, hiring employees, and looks more professional than giving out your SSN

## Surety Bonds

Some states require a surety bond before issuing your contractor license. A bond protects consumers if you fail to complete a job or violate regulations.

**How bonds work:**
- You pay 1-5% of the bond amount annually (based on your credit score)
- If you have good credit, a $15,000 bond might cost $150-$450/year
- Bad credit = higher premiums

**Example state bond requirements:**
| State | Bond Amount |
|-------|-------------|
| California | $25,000 |
| Washington | $15,000-$30,000 |
| Arizona | $9,000-$100,000 (varies by project size) |
| Georgia | $25,000 minimum |

*Source: Markup and Profit State Bond Guide; Lance Surety Bonds*

# Section 3: Insurance Checklist

Insurance is one of the best investments you'll make. It protects your business, your personal assets, and gives customers confidence in hiring you.

Most clients expect proof of insurance before hiring a contractor, having it ready puts you ahead of the competition.

## Required: General Liability Insurance

**What it covers:** Property damage, bodily injury, personal injury claims arising from your work.

**Coverage amount:** $1 million per occurrence / $2 million aggregate (standard minimum)

**Typical cost:** $82-$150/month depending on trade and location

**Who requires it:** Almost every client, general contractor, and property manager.

*Source: MoneyGeek Contractor Insurance Report (2026); Insureon*

---

## Required If You Have Employees: Workers Compensation

**What it covers:** Medical expenses and lost wages if an employee is injured on the job.

**When required:** Most states require WC as soon as you have 1 employee. Some states require it at 3+ employees.

**Special cases:** California requires workers comp even for solo contractors in certain high-risk trades (roofing, HVAC, concrete, tree service).

**Typical cost:** $142-$254/month depending on trade and payroll

*Source: Industry insurance data (2025-2026); California CSLB*

---

## Required If Using Vehicle for Work: Commercial Auto Insurance

**What it covers:** Accidents while driving to/from job sites, hauling equipment, etc.

**Why you need it:** Personal auto policies typically exclude business use. If you get in an accident while driving to a job, your personal policy may deny the claim.

**Typical cost:** $80-$200/month

---

## Recommended: Tools & Equipment Coverage (Inland Marine)

*"Inland Marine" is the industry term - that's what to ask for when you call an agent.*

**What it covers:** Your tools and equipment if stolen, damaged, or lost.

**Typical cost:** $25-$75/month

**Worth it if:** You have $10,000+ in tools (they add up fast).

---

## Insurance Checklist

- [ ] Get quotes from at least 3 insurance providers (prices vary wildly)
- [ ] Purchase general liability insurance ($1M minimum)
- [ ] Get commercial auto insurance if using your vehicle for work
- [ ] Check if your state requires workers comp for solo contractors

**What to tell the insurance agent:**
1. Your trade and typical job types
2. Estimated annual revenue (even if $0 to start)
3. Whether you have or will have employees
4. What equipment/tools you own
5. Your service area

# Section 4: Money Setup

**Master this section and you'll be ahead of most contractors.**

Cash flow is the #1 thing that keeps contractors thriving. The good news? It's not complicated once you understand it.

In construction, payment cycles are longer than most industries, sometimes 60-90 days. That's just how the industry works. The contractors who succeed are the ones who plan for it.

---

## Bank Accounts (A Great First Step)

### Business Checking Account
**Why:** Keep business money completely separate from personal money.

**Why this matters:**
- Keeping things separate makes bookkeeping SO much easier
- Tax time becomes simple instead of stressful
- You'll always know exactly how your business is doing
- It looks professional if you ever need a loan or get audited

**How to open:** Bring your EIN, LLC documents, and ID to any bank. Many offer free business checking.

### Business Savings Account
**Why:** Set aside money for taxes and emergencies.

**Rule of thumb:** Transfer 25-30% of every payment you receive into savings for taxes. You'll thank yourself when tax time comes.

---

## Accounting System

You don't need anything fancy. You need a system.

**Options:**
- **QuickBooks Self-Employed:** $15/month - good for solo contractors
- **Wave:** Free - basic but functional
- **Spreadsheet:** $0 - simple and effective

**What to track from day one:**
- Every dollar that comes in
- Every dollar that goes out
- Categorize expenses (materials, fuel, insurance, tools, etc.)

---

## Emergency Fund Target

How much cash should you keep in reserve?

| Risk Level | Emergency Fund Target |
|------------|----------------------|
| Conservative (no existing clients) | 12 months operating expenses |
| Moderate (some existing clients) | 6 months operating expenses |
| Minimum viable | 3 months operating expenses |

**Operating expenses include:** Insurance, vehicle payment, fuel, phone, subscriptions, loan payments, and minimum personal expenses (rent, food, etc.)

---

## Payment Terms

Establish your payment terms before your first job:

### Small Jobs (<$2,000)
- Due on completion
- Cash, check, or card accepted

### Medium Jobs ($2,000-$10,000)
- 50% deposit to start
- 50% due on completion

### Large Jobs (>$10,000)
- 30-50% deposit to start
- Progress payments at milestones (25% at rough-in, etc.)
- 10% due on final walkthrough

**Note:** Some states regulate deposit amounts. California, for example, caps home improvement deposits at $1,000 or 10% of the contract price, whichever is less.

---

## How You'll Accept Payments

| Method | Pros | Cons | Fees |
|--------|------|------|------|
| Check | No fees, familiar | Slow (1-2 day deposit), can bounce | $0 |
| ACH/Bank Transfer | Low fees, fast | Requires client's bank info | ~0.8% |
| Credit Card | Convenient, fast payment | Higher fees | 2.6-2.9% |
| Cash | Instant, no fees | Must track for taxes, risky | $0 |

---

## The Cash Flow Mindset

**Invoice the same day you finish.** The sooner you send it, the sooner you get paid. Make it a habit.

**Follow up on overdue invoices.** A friendly check-in 3-5 days after the due date usually does the trick.

**Protect your time.** If a client hasn't paid for job #1, it's okay to wait before starting job #2 for them.

**Use payment milestones on bigger jobs.** For larger projects, bill at stages (rough-in, drywall, finish, final) so you're not waiting until the end to get paid.

# Section 6: Your First 10 Customers

You don't need a marketing budget. You need 10 people who trust you.

---

## The Friends & Family Launch

Your first 5-10 jobs will likely come from people who already know you. That's not cheating, that's smart business.

**Action steps:**
1. Make a list of everyone you know who owns a home or business
2. Tell them you're starting your contracting business
3. Ask: "Do you know anyone who needs [electrical/plumbing/HVAC/etc.] work?"
4. Optional: Offer a "friends and family" rate for your first few jobs

**Tip:** Even for friends and family, charge something. It values your skills and sets healthy expectations from the start.

---

## The Neighborhood Effect

One of the best marketing strategies costs nothing: do great work, and neighbors notice.

When you're working on a job:
- Your truck is parked outside (free advertising)
- Neighbors see you working
- They walk by and ask what you're doing

**Be proactive:**
- Carry business cards in your pocket
- Introduce yourself to neighbors who walk by
- Ask your customer: "Would you mind if I left a few cards with your neighbors?"

**The "while you're here" opportunity:**
Many contractors report that their most profitable days come from "while you're here" jobs, a neighbor sees you working, asks for a quick estimate, and you do two jobs in one trip.

*Pro tip: Having a quoting app on your phone (like QuoteCat) lets you send a professional estimate on the spot before the neighbor forgets or calls someone else.*

---

## Pricing Your First Jobs

Here's something that might feel counterintuitive: **you don't need to be the cheapest to get work.**

It's tempting to undercut everyone when you're starting out. But experienced contractors will tell you: the clients who hire based on price alone aren't the clients you want anyway.

**A better approach:**
- Calculate what you'd earn as an employee (hourly wage)
- Your rate should be AT LEAST 2x that (to cover overhead, taxes, and profit)
- If quoting a price feels slightly uncomfortable, you're probably in the right range

The right clients value quality, reliability, and professionalism. Price yourself accordingly, and you'll attract better customers from day one.

*For detailed pricing strategies, see The Contractor Pricing Guide at quotecat.ai/pricing-guide*

---

## Building Reputation from Day One

- [ ] Take before/after photos of every job (you'll need these for marketing)
- [ ] Ask happy customers for Google reviews (87% of people read reviews before hiring)
- [ ] Create a Google Business Profile (free, essential)
- [ ] Respond to every review, even negative ones (shows you care)

**The best referrals happen without asking.** Do great work, leave the jobsite cleaner than you found it, and be easy to work with. Happy customers tell their friends.

When someone loves their new deck, they show it off. When their lights finally work right, they tell the neighbor who's been complaining about theirs. The outcome sells the next job.

Leave a few business cards behind, send a quick "thanks" text, and move on.

The rest happens naturally.

# Section 9: Lessons from Contractors Who've Been There

Every successful contractor picked up a few things along the way. Here's wisdom from those who came before you, so you can get there faster.

---

## Lesson #1: Charge What You're Worth from Day One

**What some new contractors think:** "I'll charge less to get the work, then raise prices later."

**What experienced contractors know:** Your early clients set expectations. Price-shoppers will always leave for someone $50 cheaper, they were never loyal customers anyway.

**The smart approach:** Calculate your true costs (labor + materials + overhead + profit) and price accordingly. The right clients will pay for quality work. With average profit margins in construction around 6% (CFMA 2024), there's no room for underpricing.

*Want a step-by-step pricing system? → The Contractor Pricing Guide at quotecat.ai/pricing-guide*

---

## Lesson #2: Put Everything in Writing

**What some new contractors think:** "We agreed on the phone, that's good enough."

**What experienced contractors know:** Memory is unreliable, for you AND the customer. Written quotes prevent 90% of disputes before they happen.

**The smart approach:** Even for small jobs, even for friends, send a simple written quote. "Install ceiling fan in master bedroom - $275, includes labor and materials." It takes 2 minutes and protects everyone.

---

## Lesson #3: Keep Business and Personal Money Separate

**What some new contractors think:** "I'll sort it out at tax time."

**What experienced contractors know:** You won't remember which gas station fill-up was for work 8 months later. Separate accounts make tax time simple.

**The smart approach:** Open a business checking account in week one. Use it for ALL business expenses. Keep personal spending completely separate. Your future self (and your accountant) will thank you.

---

## Lesson #4: It's Okay to Be Booked Out

**What some new contractors think:** "I can't say no to work!"

**What experienced contractors know:** Taking on too much leads to rushed work, mistakes, and burnout. Quality suffers, and so does your reputation.

**The smart approach:** "I'm booked out 3 weeks, I can get you on the schedule then." This isn't a problem, it's a sign of a healthy business. Scarcity actually increases your perceived value. Better to do fewer jobs excellently than many jobs poorly.

---

## Lesson #5: Track Your Time and Costs (At Least at First)

**What some new contractors think:** "I know how long things take."

**What experienced contractors know:** Most people underestimate. Tracking reveals the truth, and helps you price more accurately.

**The smart approach:** For your first 20 jobs, track hours and material costs. Then review: Did your estimates match reality? Where did you lose time? Contractors who track their numbers consistently see better profit margins than those who guess.
