# Writing Style Guide

Guidelines for QuoteCat content, based on Seph's preferences.

---

## Tone

**Conversational, not lecturing.** Write like you're talking to a friend who happens to be a contractor. You're sharing what you know, not teaching down to them.

**Acknowledge shared reality.** Use phrases like "Of course..." or "You already know..." instead of declaring facts at people. They're not stupid - they just haven't connected the dots yet.

**Realistic, not hyperbolic.** Say "hardly ever" instead of "never." Say "most contractors" instead of "all contractors." Absolutes feel preachy.

**Inviting, not commanding.** "Do you want to see if that works?" instead of "Let's see if that works." Give them agency.

---

## Word Choice

| Avoid | Prefer |
|-------|--------|
| never | hardly ever, rarely |
| always | usually, most of the time |
| Let's... | Here's how... / Do you want to... |
| You need to... | Consider... / Here's why... |
| Right now, do X | When you're ready, do X |
| a different approach | our options (collaborative) |
| paperwork | boring paperwork (relatable specifics) |
| countless hours | countless maintenance hours (specific) |
| costs you thousands | costs you real money (personal, no cap) |
| X matters—Y is higher | X matters, and Y is higher (flows as one thought) |

---

## Punctuation

**Commas over em dashes.** Em dashes (—) are fine occasionally for contrast, but don't squish them between words. When in doubt, use a comma or period.

**Good em dash use:**
- Contrast: "I'm asking what it costs you—not what you charge."
- Lists after a statement: "You're taking on risk—callbacks, slow seasons, bad clients."

**Bad em dash use:**
- "eat—you're paying yourself" → should be comma
- "suddenly—you've done 20% more" → should be comma or period

**Colons for setup.** When introducing an explanation or example, or before a list: "Here's the thing: hesitation kills deals." Or: "you're taking on risks: callbacks, slow seasons, bad clients."

---

## Structure

**Let key points breathe.** Don't cram impact into one paragraph. Payoff lines get their own space. Use commas to create beats.

Bad:
> That's $8,000 you're leaving on the table. Every job. If you do 15 jobs a year, that's $120,000.

Good:
> That's $8,000 you're leaving on the table.
>
> Every job.
>
> If you do 15 jobs a year, that's **$120,000** you're giving away.

Another example:
> Instead, you're running a business and taking on risks: callbacks, slow seasons, bad clients, liability.
>
> You deserve a return on that risk.

**Bold headers should stand alone.** Don't squish them into the paragraph.

Bad:
> **Callbacks:** Every callback costs you twice...

Good:
> **Callbacks**
>
> Every callback costs you twice...

---

## Numbers

**Use human time.** "20-30 minutes" not "1.3-1.5 hours." People don't think in decimals.

**Round when it doesn't matter.** "$40,000" not "$39,600" - close enough, easier to remember.

**Be specific when it adds punch.** "$120,000" hits harder than "over $100K."

---

## Questions

**Ask real questions, not rhetorical ones.** If you're asking a question, it should feel like you actually want them to think about the answer.

**Don't answer your own question immediately.** Let it sit for a beat.

Bad:
> What's your markup? If you said 20%...

Good:
> What's your markup?
>
> If you said 20%, you're in good company.

---

## Celebration, Not Lecturing

Exclamation points can work when they're celebratory, not pushy.

Bad:
> You need to do this!

Good:
> That's your number!

The difference: one is barking, the other is high-fiving.

**Playful references are good.** "It's a Trap!" beats "The Trap" - adds personality, winks at the reader. "Your Homework, If You Choose To Accept It" instead of just "Your Homework" - gives agency and adds a pop culture nod.

---

## Things to Avoid

- Starting sentences with "Here's a question:"—just ask it
- "Quick quiz:" or "Quick question:"—just get to it
- Excessive "Let's" (feels like herding)
- Telling them what they "should" do (preachy)
- Rhetorical questions that don't land
- Fictional characters with specific details that might not be true

---

## The Vibe

You're a contractor who figured this stuff out the hard way, sharing what you learned with someone who's a few years behind you. You're not a consultant. You're not a guru. You're just someone who's been there.

Respect their intelligence. They're skilled at their trade—they just never learned the business side. That's not their fault.
