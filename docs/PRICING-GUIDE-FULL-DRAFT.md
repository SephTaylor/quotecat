# The Contractor Pricing Guide

**How to Price Jobs Profitably, Stop Leaving Money on the Table, and Keep More of What You Earn**

---

# Introduction: Why Most Contractors Underprice

Let's start with an uncomfortable truth: most contractors are working too hard for too little money.

You're good at your trade. You show up on time, do quality work, and take care of your customers. But at the end of the year, you look at your bank account and wonder where it all went.

Here's what usually happened: you underpriced your jobs.

Not by accident. Not because you're bad at math. But because nobody ever taught you how to price work correctly. Trade school teaches you how to wire a panel or sweat a pipe. It doesn't teach you how to run a profitable business.

**The "I just want to win the job" trap**

When you're starting out, you think the goal is to stay busy. So you bid low to win jobs. You figure you'll make it up on volume.

But here's the math problem: if you're losing $500 on every job, doing more jobs just means losing more money. Volume doesn't fix bad pricing—it accelerates the problem.

According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, only about 36% of construction businesses that started in 2011 were still operating by 2022. Construction has the sharpest first-year decline of any industry—24% of new construction businesses fail in their first year alone.

> **Source:** U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, Establishment Age and Survival Data (2024)

**Why racing to the bottom kills your business**

There will always be someone willing to do it cheaper. Always. Usually it's someone who:
- Doesn't have insurance
- Doesn't pay taxes properly
- Doesn't understand their true costs
- Will be out of business in 18 months

You can't compete with someone who's pricing themselves into bankruptcy. And you shouldn't try.

The contractors who last 10, 20, 30 years aren't the cheapest. They're the ones who charge enough to:
- Pay themselves a real salary
- Cover all their overhead
- Have money for slow seasons
- Invest in better tools and training
- Actually enjoy their work

**The goal of this guide**

This guide will teach you how to charge what you're worth AND win jobs. Those two things aren't opposites—they go together.

When you price correctly:
- You attract better clients (cheap clients are usually the most demanding)
- You do better work (because you're not rushing to the next job)
- You can afford to stand behind your work
- You build a business instead of just having a job

Let's get started.

## Chapter 1: Your Real Costs (They're Higher Than You Think)

Before you can price a job, you need to know what it actually costs you to do that job. Not what you think it costs. What it *actually* costs.

Most contractors drastically underestimate their costs because they only count the obvious stuff: materials and labor. But there's a lot more eating into your profit.

### Direct Costs

These are the costs directly tied to a specific job:

**Materials**
- Everything you buy for the job
- Don't forget: fasteners, consumables, tape, wire nuts, flux, fittings
- Delivery fees if applicable
- Returns/restocking fees when you overbuy

**Labor**
- Your time (yes, your time has a cost—we'll calculate it in Chapter 2)
- Employee wages
- Payroll taxes: Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), federal and state unemployment (varies by state)
- Workers comp insurance (varies significantly by trade—electricians typically pay 3-5% of payroll, while roofers can pay 15-25% or more due to higher risk)

**Subcontractors**
- What you pay subs
- Any markup you need to add for coordination/liability

### Indirect Costs (Overhead)

These are the costs of running your business, whether you have jobs or not:

**Vehicle**
- Truck payment or lease
- Insurance
- Gas (track this—it's probably more than you think)
- Maintenance, tires, repairs
- Registration, inspection

Most contractors spend $800-1,500/month on a work truck when you add up payment, insurance, gas, and maintenance.

**Tools & Equipment**
- Tool purchases and replacements
- Tool maintenance and repair
- Rental equipment when needed
- Batteries, blades, bits (consumables)

**Insurance**
- General liability (typically $1,500-3,000/year for small contractors, more for high-risk trades)
- Vehicle insurance (if not counted above)
- Workers comp (if not in labor)
- Professional liability/E&O (if applicable)

**Office & Admin**
- Phone and internet
- Software subscriptions (accounting, estimating, scheduling)
- Office supplies
- Accounting/bookkeeping fees
- Legal fees

**Licenses & Certifications**
- Contractor license renewal
- Trade certifications (continuing education)
- Permits (if you eat these rather than billing them)
- Association memberships

**Marketing**
- Website hosting
- Google Ads or other advertising
- Truck lettering/wraps
- Business cards, yard signs
- Lead services (if you use them)

### Hidden Costs (The Ones That Kill You)

**Callbacks and warranty work**
Every callback costs you twice: once for the time, and again for the materials. And you can't bill for it.

Budget 2-3% of revenue for warranty/callback work. Experienced contractors report callback rates anywhere from 2-5% depending on trade and job complexity. If you're not tracking this, start.

**Unbilled time**
- Driving to estimates that don't close
- Phone calls and emails
- Picking up materials
- Dealing with inspectors
- Administrative work
- Chasing payments

For every 8 hours of billable work, most contractors have 2-4 hours of unbilled time. Track yours for a week—you might be surprised.

**Bad weather / slow seasons**
If you're in a seasonal trade, you need to make enough during busy season to cover slow season. A plumber in Minnesota might only have 9-10 productive months. That means those months need to cover 12 months of overhead.

**Mistakes and redo's**
Everyone makes mistakes. Measure twice, cut once—but sometimes you still cut wrong. Budget for it.

**Collection problems**
Not every invoice gets paid on time. Some don't get paid at all. If you're collecting 95% of what you bill (which is typical for well-managed residential contractors), you need to price 5% higher to compensate.

### The True Cost Worksheet

Add up all your overhead costs for a year:

| Category | Annual Cost |
|----------|-------------|
| Vehicle (payment, insurance, gas, maintenance) | $_______ |
| Tools & equipment | $_______ |
| Insurance (GL, WC, etc.) | $_______ |
| Office & admin | $_______ |
| Licenses & certifications | $_______ |
| Marketing | $_______ |
| Callbacks & warranty (estimate 2-3% of revenue) | $_______ |
| Bad debt (estimate 2-5% of revenue) | $_______ |
| **TOTAL ANNUAL OVERHEAD** | $_______ |

Now divide by the number of billable hours you work per year.

If you work 2,000 hours a year but only 1,500 are billable, use 1,500.

**Your overhead cost per billable hour:** Total overhead ÷ billable hours = $______/hour

This is the number most contractors don't know. And it's why they underprice.

## Chapter 2: Setting Your Labor Rate

Here's a question that makes contractors uncomfortable: How much should you pay yourself?

Not how much you take home. How much your time is worth as a cost to the business.

### The $50/Hour Myth

Many contractors think: "I want to make $100,000 a year. That's about $50/hour. So I'll charge $50/hour for labor."

This is wrong, and here's why:

$50/hour × 2,000 hours = $100,000

But you don't work 2,000 billable hours. Most contractors work 1,400-1,600 billable hours per year, with the rest eaten by unbilled time—travel, estimates, admin, material runs.

And $50/hour doesn't include:
- Your overhead (from Chapter 1)
- Your profit margin (yes, you need profit on top of salary)
- Payroll taxes if you're paying yourself as an employee
- Benefits (health insurance, retirement)

### The Real Formula

**Step 1: What do you want to earn?**

Pick a number. Let's say $100,000/year in salary.

**Step 2: Add the cost of benefits**

Health insurance: $6,000-$15,000/year (if self-employed, depending on coverage level and family size)
Retirement contributions: 10-15% of salary = $10,000-$15,000
Payroll taxes (if applicable): ~7.65% = $7,650

Let's call it $25,000 in benefits.

Total compensation cost: $125,000

**Step 3: Add your overhead allocation**

From Chapter 1, let's say your overhead is $60,000/year.

Running total: $185,000

**Step 4: Add your profit margin**

Your business needs to make a profit beyond paying you. This is what builds the business, covers unexpected expenses, and rewards you for the risk of being a business owner.

According to CFMA's 2024 Construction Financial Benchmarker:
- **Industry average net profit:** 6.3% (up from 5% in 2022)
- **Healthy target:** 8-10%
- **Best in class (top 25%):** 11.9%

Target at least 10-15% net profit margin. At 15% margin, you need: $185,000 ÷ 0.85 = $217,647

> **Source:** CFMA 2024 Construction Financial Benchmarker; NAHB Remodeler Survey

**Step 5: Divide by billable hours**

If you work 1,500 billable hours per year:

$217,647 ÷ 1,500 = **$145/hour**

That's your real labor rate. Not $50. $145.

### "But nobody will pay $145/hour!"

Two responses:

1. Some will. The clients who value quality, reliability, and professionalism will pay it. The ones who won't weren't your customers anyway.

2. You don't have to quote hourly. Most contractors quote by the job. The client sees "$3,200 to upgrade your panel," not "$145/hour × 22 hours." (More on this in Chapter 5.)

### What Others Charge

For reference, here are typical labor rates by trade (what contractors charge clients, not employee wages):

| Trade | Typical Hourly Rate | Notes |
|-------|---------------------|-------|
| **Electrician** | $75-150/hour | Apprentice $50-75, journeyman $75-100, master $100-150+ |
| **Plumber** | $80-150/hour | Residential avg $80-130, master plumber $100-200 |
| **HVAC Technician** | $75-150/hour | Service calls often $100-250 with trip fee included |
| **General Contractor** | $50-150/hour | Usually charge % of project (10-20%) rather than hourly |
| **Carpenter** | $40-100/hour | Framing $40-60, finish/trim $50-100 |
| **Painter** | $25-75/hour | Standard work $25-50, detailed/specialty $75-100 |
| **Roofer** | $50-100/hour | Often quote per square ($150-400) rather than hourly |

> **Sources:** HomeGuide, Angi, HomeAdvisor, Housecall Pro pricing guides (2025-2026 data)

**Regional variations are significant.** East and West Coast contractors charge 20-40% more than the national average. California electricians are 15-25% above national rates. Texas metro areas (Austin, Dallas) have seen 15-20% increases since 2022 due to construction booms.

**Emergency/after-hours:** Expect to pay 1.5x to 2x the standard rate for nights, weekends, and holidays.

### Hourly vs. Day Rate vs. Project Rate

**Hourly** works for:
- Service calls
- Time & materials jobs
- Repair work where scope is uncertain

**Day rate** works for:
- Longer projects where hourly tracking is tedious
- When you want simplicity
- Typically 8 hours × hourly rate, sometimes with a small discount

**Project rate (flat rate)** works for:
- Jobs where you can accurately estimate time
- When clients want price certainty
- Usually more profitable (you keep the savings if you're efficient)

More on flat rate vs. T&M in Chapter 5.

## Chapter 3: Markup vs. Margin (Know the Difference)

This is where contractors get confused—and leave money on the table.

Markup and margin are NOT the same thing. Understanding the difference can add tens of thousands of dollars to your bottom line.

### Markup: What You Add

Markup is the percentage you add to your cost.

**Example:**
- Your cost: $1,000
- Markup: 30%
- Selling price: $1,000 + $300 = $1,300

### Margin: What You Keep

Margin is the percentage of the selling price that's profit.

**Example:**
- Selling price: $1,300
- Your cost: $1,000
- Gross profit: $300
- Margin: $300 ÷ $1,300 = **23%**

See the problem? You added 30%, but you only kept 23%.

### The Markup/Margin Conversion

Here's the math that trips people up:

| If Your Markup Is... | Your Actual Margin Is... |
|----------------------|--------------------------|
| 10% | 9.1% |
| 20% | 16.7% |
| 25% | 20% |
| 30% | 23% |
| 33% | 25% |
| 40% | 28.6% |
| 50% | 33.3% |
| 100% | 50% |

**The formula:**
- Markup to Margin: Markup ÷ (1 + Markup) = Margin
- Margin to Markup: Margin ÷ (1 - Margin) = Markup

**Example:** You want a 30% profit margin. What markup do you need?

0.30 ÷ (1 - 0.30) = 0.30 ÷ 0.70 = 42.9% markup

You need to mark up by ~43% to achieve a 30% margin.

### Recommended Markups by Trade

These are starting points—adjust based on your market and overhead:

| Category | Typical Markup |
|----------|----------------|
| **Materials** | 15-30% |
| **Subcontractor work** | 10-20% |
| **Labor** | Already built into your rate |
| **Overall job (T&M)** | 35-50% on total cost |
| **Overall job (flat rate)** | 50-100% on cost |

Why higher markup on flat-rate jobs? Because you're taking on the risk. If the job runs long, you eat it. That risk has value.

### Finding Your Target Markup

Work backwards from your desired profit:

**Step 1:** Determine your target net profit margin

Industry benchmarks (CFMA 2024):
- **6%** = industry average (you're surviving)
- **8-10%** = healthy, well-managed business
- **12%+** = best in class (top 25% of contractors)

**Gross profit margin benchmarks:**
- General contractors: 12-16%
- Specialty contractors (electrical, plumbing, HVAC): 15-25%
- Residential remodeling: 18-25%
- Best in class: 25%+

> **Source:** CFMA 2024 Construction Financial Benchmarker

**Step 2:** Add your overhead percentage
- Calculate: Annual overhead ÷ Annual revenue = Overhead %
- Example: $60,000 overhead ÷ $400,000 revenue = 15%

**Step 3:** Calculate required gross margin
- Gross margin needed = Net profit + Overhead
- Example: 15% profit + 15% overhead = 30% gross margin

**Step 4:** Convert to markup
- 30% margin = 43% markup (using formula above)

So you need to mark up every job by at least 43% just to hit 15% profit.

### The Real-World Application

Let's price a bathroom remodel:

| Item | Your Cost | Markup | Sell Price |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Materials | $4,000 | 25% | $5,000 |
| Labor (your time, 60 hrs @ $60/hr cost) | $3,600 | 50% | $5,400 |
| Subcontractor (plumber) | $1,200 | 15% | $1,380 |
| Permits | $350 | 0% | $350 |
| **Total** | $9,150 | | **$12,130** |

Overall markup: 32.5%
Overall margin: 24.6%

If your overhead is 15% of revenue, your net profit on this job is about 9.6%.

To hit 15% net profit, you'd need to increase prices by about $700—either higher markups or a flat "project fee."

## Chapter 5: Flat Rate vs. Time & Materials

Every contractor faces this choice: Do I quote a fixed price, or do I bill for time and materials as I go?

Both have their place. Here's when to use each.

### When Flat Rate Wins

**Predictable jobs with known scope:**
- Standard service calls (diagnose + repair)
- Equipment replacements (water heater, AC unit, panel)
- Routine installations (ceiling fan, garbage disposal, outlet)

**When the client wants certainty:**
- Insurance work (they need a number)
- Budget-conscious clients who need to plan
- Commercial work with purchase orders

**When you're efficient:**
- You've done this job 100 times
- You know exactly how long it takes
- Your efficiency becomes profit

**The flat rate advantage:**
Contractors who switch to flat rate pricing consistently report higher profit margins. If a water heater swap takes you 3 hours but you quoted based on 4 hours, you just gave yourself a raise. Your efficiency is rewarded.

### When T&M Wins

**Unpredictable jobs:**
- Old homes with unknown conditions
- Troubleshooting and diagnostics
- Repair work where you don't know the extent

**When scope keeps changing:**
- Remodels where the client "adds one more thing"
- Discovery work (open the wall, find surprises)

**When you're new to a job type:**
- First time doing this specific work
- Unfamiliar building or system

**The T&M advantage:**
If you open a wall and find knob-and-tube wiring, wet rot, and a family of mice, you're not stuck eating the cost.

### The Hybrid Approach (Best of Both Worlds)

Quote flat rate for the defined scope. Bill T&M for discoveries.

**Example language:**

> "This quote covers removing and replacing your water heater with a 50-gallon gas unit. If we discover any issues with your gas line, venting, or water supply that require additional work, we'll discuss options and pricing before proceeding. Additional work is billed at $150/hour plus materials."

This protects you while giving the client price certainty for the expected work.

### How to Present T&M Without Scaring Clients

T&M makes some clients nervous. They imagine a runaway bill with no end.

**Do this:**
1. Give a realistic range: "Based on what I'm seeing, I expect this to take 4-6 hours at $125/hour, plus materials around $200-300. So you're probably looking at $700-1,050 total."

2. Set a "not to exceed" if possible: "I'll cap labor at 8 hours. If we're approaching that and not done, I'll stop and we'll discuss."

3. Communicate during the job: "Good news—we're about halfway through and on track. Should be wrapping up in about 2 more hours."

4. Itemize the invoice clearly: Show exactly what you did, how long it took, and what materials you used. No surprises.

**Don't do this:**
- Show up without discussing rates
- Give no estimate at all
- Submit a surprise invoice double what they expected
- Bill for time you weren't actually working

## Chapter 6: The Psychology of Pricing

Pricing isn't just math. It's psychology. How you present a price matters as much as the number itself.

### Anchoring: The First Number Sets the Frame

The first number someone sees shapes their expectations.

This was proven in a famous 1974 study by psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman. They spun a wheel of fortune (rigged to land on either 10 or 65), then asked participants to estimate what percentage of African countries were in the United Nations. The group that saw "10" guessed 25% on average. The group that saw "65" guessed 45%. A random number they knew was meaningless still influenced their estimate.

> **Citation:** Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases. *Science*, 185(4157), 1124–1131.

**Example:**
If you show the premium option first ($16,800 HVAC system), suddenly the mid-tier ($11,200) feels reasonable. But if you show the base option first ($8,500), the mid-tier feels expensive.

**Application:**
Always present your highest-priced option first (or at least make it visible). Even if most people choose the middle, the top price makes the middle feel like a deal.

### Charm Pricing: Does $4,997 Beat $5,000?

You've seen this everywhere: $9.99 instead of $10, $2,997 instead of $3,000.

Does it work? The research says: it depends.

A 2003 study by MIT and University of Chicago tested prices of $34, $39, and $44 for the same item. The $39 price sold best—even though it wasn't the cheapest. Consumers picked the 9-ending price. A 2024 meta-analysis of 69 studies found charm prices increase purchase decisions by about 13% on average.

> **Citation:** Stiving, M., & Winer, R.S. (1997). An Empirical Analysis of Price Endings with Scanner Data. *Journal of Consumer Research*, 24(1), 57-67.

**For small purchases:** Yes, charm pricing works. $9.99 feels cheaper than $10. Studies show consumers anchor on the left digit, so $9.99 "feels like" $9.

**For large purchases (construction work):** Less clear. A sophisticated client might see $4,997 as gimmicky. A $5,000 round number can feel more professional and signal quality.

**My recommendation:** Use round numbers for professional services. $5,000 or $4,900—not $4,997. You're not a used car lot.

### The Decoy Effect

When you offer three options, the middle one becomes most attractive—especially if you design it that way.

This was discovered by researchers Huber, Payne, and Puto in 1982. They gave participants a choice: a five-star restaurant 25 minutes away, or a three-star restaurant 5 minutes away. About equal preference. Then they added a decoy: a four-star restaurant 35 minutes away (worse than the five-star in every way). Suddenly, people preferred the five-star restaurant. The decoy made it look better by comparison.

> **Citation:** Huber, J., Payne, J. W., & Puto, C. (1982). Adding Asymmetrically Dominated Alternatives: Violations of Regularity and the Similarity Hypothesis. *Journal of Consumer Research*, 9(1), 90-98.

Behavioral economist Dan Ariely popularized this in his book *Predictably Irrational* (2008) with The Economist subscription experiment: when they offered web-only ($59) and print+web ($125), most chose web-only. When they added print-only ($125)—the same price as print+web—suddenly 84% chose print+web. The print-only option was a decoy.

**Classic example:**
- Small popcorn: $4
- Large popcorn: $7

Most people buy small.

Add a medium:
- Small: $4
- Medium: $6.50
- Large: $7

Now most people buy large. The medium makes the large look like a better deal.

**In construction:**
Your "Better" tier isn't just an option—it's a tool to make "Best" look reasonable or make "Better" the obvious choice, depending on how you price the gaps.

### Confidence: The Ultimate Pricing Tool

Here's the thing about pricing: hesitation kills deals.

If you quote $8,500 but you sound uncertain—"Um, I think it would be around, uh, maybe $8,500 or so?"—the client hears: "He's not sure. Maybe I can get a lower price."

If you quote $8,500 with confidence—"This job is $8,500. That includes everything we discussed: materials, labor, permits, and a 3-year warranty on our workmanship."—the client hears: "He knows what he's doing."

**Confidence comes from:**
- Knowing your numbers (Chapters 1-3)
- Having a system (Good/Better/Best, flat rate pricing)
- Experience (you've done this before)
- Believing in your value (you're worth it)

The same $8,500 quote closes at very different rates depending on how you deliver it.

## Chapter 7: The Anatomy of a Winning Quote

A quote isn't just a price. It's a sales document, a legal document, and a trust-building tool. Do it right, and you close more jobs at better prices.

### What to Include

**1. Your branding**
- Company name and logo
- License number
- Contact info
- Professional appearance matters

**2. Client information**
- Name and address
- Job site address (if different)
- Contact info

**3. Date and quote number**
- For your records and theirs
- Makes you look organized

**4. Scope of work**
- Detailed description of what you'll do
- Be specific enough that there's no confusion
- But not so detailed that you're writing a novel

**5. What's included**
- Materials (be specific: "50-gallon Rheem water heater, model XYZ")
- Labor
- Permits (or note that permits are client's responsibility)
- Cleanup
- Warranty terms

**6. What's NOT included**
- This is critical for avoiding disputes
- "Does not include: electrical upgrades, drywall repair, painting"

**7. Price breakdown (optional)**
- Some clients want to see materials vs. labor
- Others just want a total
- Know your audience

**8. The total price**
- Clear and prominent
- If using Good/Better/Best, show all three options clearly

**9. Terms and conditions**
- Payment terms (due on completion, 50% deposit, etc.)
- Change order policy
- Warranty information
- Expiration date

**10. Signature lines**
- Space for them to sign and date
- Makes it a binding agreement when signed

### What to Leave Out

**Detailed cost breakdowns with your markup visible:**
Don't show: "Materials: $1,000 + 25% markup = $1,250"
Do show: "Materials: $1,250"

**Hourly rates (on flat-rate quotes):**
If you're quoting a project price, don't break down the hours. The client will do math and decide you're "too expensive per hour" when that's not the point.

**Uncertainty:**
"I think this should cover it" or "Prices subject to change" (excessive hedging)

### Professional Formatting

Your quote competes against other contractors' quotes. The one that looks most professional often wins—even if it's not the cheapest. Clients equate a professional-looking quote with professional work.

**Minimum standards:**
- Clean, readable layout
- Your logo
- Consistent font (nothing Comic Sans)
- No handwritten scribbles on napkins
- PDF format (not just text in an email)

**Extra credit:**
- Photos of similar completed work
- Material spec sheets attached
- Clear visual hierarchy (the price is easy to find)
- Mobile-friendly format

### Terms That Protect You

**Payment terms:**
- "50% deposit due upon acceptance. Balance due upon completion."
- Or: "Due upon completion. 2% discount for payment within 7 days."

**Change orders:**
- "Any work not specified in this quote will require a separate change order with additional pricing."
- "Verbal requests for additional work must be confirmed in writing before work begins."

**Warranty:**
- "Workmanship guaranteed for [X years]. Manufacturer warranties apply to all materials."
- "Warranty void if equipment is serviced by others."

**Price validity:**
- "This quote is valid for 30 days from issue date."
- Creates urgency without pressure
- Protects you from material price changes

## Chapter 8: Speed Wins

Here's a stat that should change how you do business:

**78% of customers purchase from the company that responds first.** Not the best. Not the cheapest. The first.

According to research by InsideSales and the Harvard Business Review:
- 35-50% of sales go to the vendor that responds first
- Responding within 5 minutes makes you 21x more likely to qualify a lead than waiting 30 minutes
- Companies responding within 1 hour are 7x more likely to qualify the lead—and 60x more likely than those waiting 24 hours

> **Sources:** Harvard Business Review Lead Response Study; InsideSales.com; MIT Lead Response Management Study (Dr. James Oldroyd)

Yet the average company takes 42-47 hours to respond to a lead. Meanwhile, 82% of consumers expect a response within 10 minutes.

Why? Because:
- Clients want their problem solved now
- Waiting creates doubt ("Will they even call back?")
- Being first demonstrates competence and reliability
- Momentum is real—the first to quote sets the standard

### The "Quote On-Site" Advantage

If you can quote during your first visit, do it. Bring:
- A tablet or phone with quoting software
- Your standard pricing for common jobs
- A calculator (or better, an app that does the math)

[SIDEBAR: This is where software like QuoteCat pays for itself—build and send a professional quote before you leave the driveway.]

### Templates and Systems

You shouldn't be creating quotes from scratch every time. Build templates for your common jobs:

**Service call template:**
- Diagnostic fee
- Common repairs with flat-rate pricing
- "Additional work quoted separately"

**Equipment replacement template:**
- Good/Better/Best options pre-built
- Just fill in the specifics

**Remodel template:**
- Scope sections you can include/exclude
- Standard pricing for common upgrades

### Follow-Up Timing That Closes Deals

**If you quoted on-site:** Follow up the next day. "Hi [Name], wanted to check if you had any questions about the quote I left yesterday."

**If you quoted remotely:** Follow up 2-3 days later. "Just wanted to make sure you received the quote. Happy to walk through the options if helpful."

**If no response after a week:** One more follow-up. "I know you're busy. If you've decided to go another direction, no worries—I'd just appreciate a quick heads up so I can close out the estimate."

**Don't do:**
- Call every day
- Act desperate
- Bad-mouth competitors
- Lower your price without being asked

## Chapter 9: Handling Price Objections

No matter how good your pricing, you'll hear objections. Here's how to handle the most common ones.

### "That's more than I expected."

**Don't say:** "Well, what were you expecting?" (puts them on defense)

**Do say:** "I understand—these projects can add up. Let me walk you through what's included so you can see where the investment goes."

Then: Review the scope, emphasize value (warranty, quality materials, your experience), and ask which parts are most important to them. If needed, offer a scaled-back option.

### "Can you do better on price?"

**Translation:** "I want to feel like I got a deal."

**Option 1 - Hold your price:**
"This is my best price for the scope we discussed. I've priced it fairly, and I stand behind the work. If budget is a concern, we could look at phasing the project or adjusting the scope."

**Option 2 - Small concession that isn't your price:**
"I can't go lower on the price, but I can throw in [free service call for a year / extended warranty / removal of old equipment] if you sign today."

**Option 3 - Reduce scope:**
"I can bring the price down if we go with the standard fixtures instead of the upgraded ones. That would save about $800. Would that work for you?"

### "The other guy was cheaper."

**Don't say:** "You get what you pay for" (insulting to them), or "He's probably unlicensed" (sounds defensive).

**Do say:** "I can't speak to their pricing, but I can tell you what's included in mine: [list key differentiators—warranty, insurance, specific materials, your experience]. Are you comparing the same scope of work?"

Often, cheaper quotes leave things out. Help them see that.

**Also valid:** "If their quote works for you, I understand. But if you run into issues down the road, give me a call—I'm happy to help."

Play the long game. Sometimes the cheap contractor burns them, and they come back.

### "Let me think about it."

**Translation:** Could mean anything. Get specific.

**Say:** "Of course—it's a big decision. Is there anything specific you're unsure about, or any information I could provide that would help?"

This opens the door to uncover the real objection. Maybe it's:
- Price (you can address it)
- Timing (you can adjust schedule)
- Spouse needs to approve (you can offer to explain to both)
- They're waiting on another quote (fair—follow up in a few days)

**Don't do:** Just accept "I'll think about it" and walk away. That's not a real answer, and you lose the ability to address concerns.

### "Just give me a ballpark."

Clients love this question. It's a trap.

**The risk:** You say "$3,000-4,000" without seeing the job. The job turns out to be $6,000. They feel like you tried to bait-and-switch them.

**Options:**

**Option 1 - Decline gracefully:**
"I'd be guessing without seeing the job, and I don't want to give you a number that turns out to be wrong. Can we set up a time for me to take a look?"

**Option 2 - Give a wide range with caveats:**
"Without seeing it, similar jobs run anywhere from $2,500 to $7,000 depending on [specific factors]. I'd need to look at yours to give you a real number."

**Option 3 - Quote sight-unseen with protection:**
"Based on what you've described, I'd estimate $4,000-5,000. But I need to see the job to confirm—things like [potential complications] could affect that."

## Chapter 10: Change Orders Done Right

Change orders are where profit goes to die—if you handle them wrong.

**Scope creep** is when the job keeps getting bigger, but your price stays the same. "While you're here, can you also..." is the most expensive phrase in construction.

### The Change Order Conversation

**When the client asks for something extra:**

1. **Acknowledge:** "Sure, we can definitely do that."

2. **Clarify:** "Just so I understand—you want [repeat what they asked for]?"

3. **Price it:** "That would be an additional $X. Want me to write that up?"

4. **Document:** Get it in writing. Email works. "Per our conversation, you've approved the additional work to [scope] for [$X]. Total project price is now [$Y]."

**Script:**
> "Hey, just want to make sure we're on the same page. Adding that outlet wasn't in the original scope, so it'll be an extra $350. If that works, I can add it to the job—just need a quick OK from you before I proceed."

### Documentation That Protects You

**The change order should include:**
- Original scope reference
- New/additional work description
- Additional price
- Revised total
- Client signature or written approval

Even an email confirmation works. The key is having something in writing before you do the work.

**If they don't approve in writing:**
Don't do the work. Politely hold firm: "I'm happy to do it—just need a quick confirmation so my records are straight."

### Getting Paid for Extras Without Awkwardness

Most clients are reasonable. They understand that extra work costs extra money. The awkwardness comes when you don't bring it up until the final invoice.

**Make it easy:**
- Bring it up immediately (not after you've done the work)
- Be matter-of-fact (it's business, not personal)
- Offer alternatives ("We can skip that if you want to keep the budget where it is")

**For bigger additions:**
Write up a mini-quote. "Here's what the additional work would involve and the cost. Let me know if you want to proceed."

## Chapter 11: The Software Fee Trap

Here's something most contractors don't think about: how much of your revenue are you giving away in software fees?

### How Other Apps Take 2.9% of Every Payment

Many contractor software platforms include payment processing. Convenient, right? But look at the fees:

**Current payment processing rates (2026):**
- **Stripe:** 2.9% + $0.30 per online transaction
- **Square:** 2.6% + $0.15 in-person, 3.3% + $0.30 online (Free plan)
- **Jobber Payments:** 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction
- **Housecall Pro:** 2.59% - 2.9% + varies (volume discounts available)

> **Sources:** Stripe.com, Square.com, Jobber pricing, Housecall Pro pricing (February 2026)

And here's what most contractors don't realize: **these platforms don't let you use cheaper processors**. They earn revenue from your transaction fees, so they lock you into their payment system.

**Do the math:**

| Annual Revenue | 2.9% Fee | Your Loss |
|----------------|----------|-----------|
| $100,000 | $2,900 | One week's pay |
| $300,000 | $8,700 | New tools |
| $500,000 | $14,500 | A helper for 3 months |

That's real money walking out the door—every year.

### What to Look For

**Payment processing:**
- Can you use your own processor (lower rates)?
- Are there additional fees on top of standard processing?
- What about ACH / bank transfers? (Stripe charges only 0.8%, capped at $5)

**Subscription fees:**
- Monthly vs. annual (usually 10-20% savings on annual)
- What's included vs. extra?
- Per-user fees that scale badly?

**Hidden costs:**
- Charges per invoice/quote sent?
- Storage limits?
- Export fees to get your own data out?

### The Principle

Your software should help you make money, not eat into every dollar you earn. Look at the total cost of ownership, not just the monthly subscription.

## Chapter 12: Getting Paid Faster

You did the work. Now get paid.

### Payment Terms That Work

**For smaller jobs (<$2,000):**
- Due on completion
- Or: Pay when invoiced (net 7 or net 15)

**For medium jobs ($2,000-10,000):**
- 50% deposit to start
- 50% due on completion

**For larger jobs (>$10,000):**
- 30-50% deposit
- Progress payments at milestones
- 10% due on completion / final walkthrough

These are common industry practices, though your state may have specific regulations on deposit amounts (California, for example, caps deposits at $1,000 or 10% for home improvement contracts).

**Why deposits matter:**
- Cash flow: Materials and payroll don't wait
- Commitment: Client with money down doesn't flake
- Protection: If they disappear, you're not fully exposed

### Invoice Timing Psychology

**Send the invoice immediately.**

Not tomorrow. Not "when you get back to the office." Today.

Why:
- Client is still happy (you just finished)
- The number is fresh in their mind
- Momentum is on your side

**Every day you wait to invoice is a day you wait to get paid.** The longer you wait, the more likely the client forgets the details—or deprioritizes paying you.

### Following Up on Overdue Invoices

**Day 1 after due date:**
"Hi [Name], just checking in—looks like payment is past due. Want to make sure you received the invoice. Any questions?"

**Day 7:**
"Following up on invoice #123. Let me know if there's an issue or if you need the invoice resent."

**Day 14:**
"This invoice is now two weeks overdue. Please let me know your plan to settle this. I'd like to avoid having to take further action."

**Day 30+:**
Time to decide: Small claims court, collections, or write it off.

**Tips:**
- Stay professional (no angry emails)
- Document every communication
- If they have a complaint about the work, address it—but don't let it be an excuse not to pay

### When to Fire a Client

Some clients aren't worth the money. Fire them if:
- They consistently pay late (more than twice)
- They dispute every invoice
- They're abusive to you or your team
- The drama isn't worth the revenue

**How to fire a client:**
"I don't think we're the best fit for your needs. I'd recommend reaching out to [other contractor] or [referral]. Best of luck with the project."

You don't owe an explanation. You don't need their permission.
