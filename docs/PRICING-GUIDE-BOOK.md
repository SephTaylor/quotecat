# Part 1: Know Your Numbers

## Chapter 1: What It Really Costs You

What does it cost you to show up at a job?

Most contractors answer with their hourly rate. "$75 an hour." Or "$100." Whatever.

But that's not what I'm asking. I'm asking what it **costs** you, not what you charge. Before you make a single dollar of profit, what does it cost just to exist as a contractor?

### The Truck Equation

Start with your truck.

- Payment or savings for replacement: $500/month
- Insurance: $200/month
- Gas: $400/month (you're probably underestimating this)
- Maintenance, tires, repairs: $200/month

That's $1,300/month just to have a truck. **$15,600 per year.**

And you need that truck whether you have jobs or not.

### The Tool Tax

Every year, tools break, wear out, or walk off the job.

- Replacements and upgrades: $2,000-4,000/year
- Batteries, blades, bits: $1,000-2,000/year

Call it **$4,000/year** to stay equipped.

### The Insurance Reality

- General liability: $2,000/year (more if you're high-risk)
- Workers comp: 3-15% of payroll depending on trade
- Vehicle (if not above): included

Let's say **$4,000/year** for a small operation.

### The Stuff Nobody Thinks About

- Phone and internet: $150/month = $1,800/year
- Software (accounting, estimating): $100/month = $1,200/year
- License renewals and continuing ed: $500/year
- Accountant: $1,500/year
- Marketing (even just basic): $2,000/year

That's another **$7,000/year**.

### The Hidden Killers

Here's where it gets ugly.

**Callbacks**

Every callback costs you twice: once for the time, again for the materials. You can't bill for it. Budget 2-3% of your revenue for warranty work.

**Unbilled time**

For every hour you bill, you're probably working another 20-30 minutes you can't charge for. Driving to estimates, picking up materials, answering calls, doing boring paperwork. That time isn't free.

**Slow seasons**

If you're in a seasonal trade, your busy months need to pay for your slow months. A Minnesota plumber might only have 9 productive months. Those 9 months need to cover 12 months of overhead.

**Mistakes**

Of course everyone screws up sometimes. Measure twice, cut once, but sometimes you still cut wrong. Budget for it.

### Add It Up

Here's a realistic overhead for a one-person operation:

| Category | Annual Cost |
|----------|-------------|
| Vehicle | $15,600 |
| Tools | $4,000 |
| Insurance | $4,000 |
| Phone, software, admin | $7,000 |
| Marketing | $2,000 |
| Callbacks (2% of $200K revenue) | $4,000 |
| Miscellaneous | $3,000 |
| **Total** | **$39,600** |

Round it to **$40,000/year** just to keep the lights on.

That's $40,000 you need to earn before you pay yourself a single dollar.

---

**The number that matters:**

Take your annual overhead and divide it by your billable hours.

If you work 2,000 hours a year but only bill 1,400 of them (the rest is unbilled time), use 1,400.

$40,000 ÷ 1,400 = **$28.57 per billable hour** just for overhead.

Every hour you work costs you almost $30 before you make anything.

Write that number down. You need it for Chapter 2.

## Chapter 2: What You Need to Charge

Now for the question everyone gets wrong: What should your hourly rate be?

The common approach is to figure out what you want to make, divide by hours, and that's your rate.

"I want $100,000. That's $50 an hour."

**This is how contractors go broke.**

### The Math That Actually Works

Here's how to build your rate from the ground up.

**Step 1: What do you want to earn?**

Be honest. Not what sounds humble. What do you actually want to take home?

Let's say $90,000. That's a reasonable living for a skilled tradesperson.

**Step 2: Add the cost of employing yourself**

If you pay yourself as an employee (which you probably should for tax reasons), add:
- Payroll taxes: ~7.65% = $6,885
- Health insurance: $8,000-15,000/year (let's say $10,000)
- Retirement contributions: 10% = $9,000

Total cost to pay yourself $90K: **$115,885**

**Step 3: Add your overhead**

From Chapter 1: $40,000

Running total: **$155,885**

**Step 4: Add profit**

"Wait, I already included my salary. Why do I need profit too?"

Because your salary is the cost of your labor. Profit is the return on your risk.

You could work for someone else, make $90K, and go home at 5pm with zero stress. Instead, you're running a business and taking on risks: callbacks, slow seasons, bad clients, liability. 

You deserve a return on that risk.

Industry benchmark: 10-15% net profit margin.

To get 15% net profit: $155,885 ÷ 0.85 = **$183,394**

**Step 5: Divide by billable hours**

$183,394 ÷ 1,400 billable hours = **$131 per hour**

Not $50. Not $75. **$131.**

---

### "But nobody around here charges that much!"

Two things:

**First:** You'd be surprised. The contractors who are actually making money aren't advertising their rates. They're quietly charging $120, $150, $180 and their clients are happy to pay because they're worth it.

**Second:** You don't have to quote hourly.

Most of the time, you're quoting projects. The client doesn't see "$131/hour x 8 hours." They see "$1,050 to install your ceiling fan." If your competitor quotes $600 for the same job but does sloppy work and won't return calls.

Guess who's actually the better deal?

## Chapter 3: Markup vs. Margin (The Difference That Costs You Real Money)

Quick quiz: You buy materials for $1,000 and mark them up 30%. What's your profit margin?

If you said 30%, you're not alone. 

But you'd be wrong.

### It's a Trap!

Markup and margin are different things. 

Confusing them is costing you, every year.

**Markup** = what you add to cost
**Margin** = what you keep from the sale

Here's the $1,000 example:
- Cost: $1,000
- 30% markup: $300
- Selling price: $1,300
- Your profit: $300
- Margin: $300 ÷ $1,300 = **23%**

You marked up 30% but only made 23%.

### The Conversion Table

Memorize this (or screenshot it):

| You Mark Up... | You Actually Keep... |
|----------------|----------------------|
| 10% | 9% |
| 20% | 17% |
| 25% | 20% |
| 30% | 23% |
| 40% | 29% |
| 50% | 33% |
| 100% | 50% |

Want a 30% margin? You need a **43% markup.**

The formula: Margin ÷ (1 - Margin) = Required Markup

30% margin: 0.30 ÷ 0.70 = 0.43 = 43% markup

---

### What This Means in Real Money

Say you do $300,000 in revenue this year. You've been using a 25% markup thinking you're making 25%.

You're actually making 20% gross margin.

If you'd used the correct 43% markup to hit 30% margin, that's an extra $30,000 in your pocket.

Same jobs. Same clients. Same hours. $30,000 more.

That's the difference between a family vacation and another year of grinding.

---

### Recommended Markups

| What You're Selling | Markup |
|---------------------|--------|
| Materials | 25-35% |
| Subcontractor work | 15-25% |
| Flat-rate jobs | 50-75% on cost |
| Time & materials | 40-50% overall |

These markups give you 20-40% gross margins. That's what you need to cover overhead and make a profit.

## Chapter 4: Good, Better, Best (Your Secret Weapon)

Here's how most contractors quote a job:

1. Look at what needs to be done
2. Add up materials and labor
3. Slap on some markup
4. Send one number

The client says yes or no. Usually no.

**There's a better way.**

### The Problem with One Price

One price turns every quote into a yes-or-no question. The client has two options: take your number or call someone else.

You have zero room to negotiate. Zero way to show value. Zero chance to upsell.

### The Solution: Three Options

Instead of one price, present three:

- **Good:** Solves the problem, basic approach
- **Better:** Solves it better, adds value (your target)
- **Best:** The premium option with everything

Now the client isn't deciding whether to hire you. They're deciding which option to pick. The psychology shifts completely.

### Why This Works (It's Science)

This isn't just a sales trick. Researchers have been studying it for decades:

**The middle option wins.** When people see three choices, they almost never pick the cheapest (feels risky) or the most expensive (feels excessive). The middle feels "just right." 60-70% of buyers pick it.

**Anchoring is real.** The first number someone sees sets their expectations. If they see $8,000 first (your Best option), suddenly $5,500 (Better) looks reasonable. Without that anchor, $5,500 might seem expensive.

**People want control.** One price feels like an ultimatum. Three prices feel like a choice. Same outcome, better feeling.

---

### How to Structure Your Tiers

Bad tiering: "Same job, just more expensive for no reason."

Good tiering: "Different solutions for different needs."

**Good (Entry):**
- Solves the immediate problem
- Standard materials and methods
- Basic warranty
- This is not a crappy option. It's legitimately good.

**Better (Your Target):**
- Solves the problem better
- Upgraded materials or methods
- Enhanced warranty
- Where 60% of clients should land

**Best (Premium):**
- The complete solution
- Best materials, longest warranty
- Future-proofing and extras
- For clients who want the best and don't mind paying

---

### Real Example: Electrical Panel Upgrade

Client needs to go from 100A to 200A service.

**Good - $3,400**
- 200A panel upgrade, code compliant
- Standard breakers
- 1-year workmanship warranty
- Gets the job done right

**Better - $4,600 ★ Most Popular**
- Everything in Good, plus:
- Arc-fault breakers where required
- Whole-house surge protector
- Panel schedule and labeling
- 3-year warranty

**Best - $6,200**
- Everything in Better, plus:
- Smart panel with app monitoring
- EV charger circuit pre-wired
- Garage sub-panel feed ready
- 5-year warranty

---

### The Math That'll Change Your Mind

Say you quote 10 panel upgrades this month.

**Old way (one price, $3,400):**
- 6 clients say yes: $20,400

**New way (three options):**
- 2 pick Good ($3,400): $6,800
- 5 pick Better ($4,600): $23,000
- 1 picks Best ($6,200): $6,200
- Total: **$36,000**

Same 8 jobs. **$15,600 more revenue.** That's 76% higher.

And you didn't work any harder. You just presented options.

---

### How to Present It

Don't email a PDF with three prices and hope they pick the middle one. Walk them through it.

**Script:**

> "I put together three options based on what you need. Let me walk you through them.
>
> The first option handles everything—it's solid, up to code, and a lot of my customers are happy with it.
>
> The second option is what I'd personally recommend for your situation. It adds [surge protection and the arc-fault breakers], which means [your electronics are protected and you meet the newer code]. Most of my clients end up going with this one.
>
> The third option is for people who want to do it once and do it right. It includes [the smart panel and the EV circuit], so if you're planning to [get an electric car or want to monitor your usage], you're set.
>
> Which one feels closest to what you're looking for?"

**Key moves:**
- Start with Good (establish the baseline)
- Recommend Better explicitly
- Mention "most of my clients" pick Better (social proof)
- Let them choose (autonomy)
- Shut up and wait for their answer

---

### Common Mistakes

**Gap too big between tiers**

If Good is $3,000 and Better is $7,000, you've lost them. Keep it 25-40% between tiers.


**Padding with junk**

Adding a $50 item to justify a $1,000 jump insults their intelligence. Each tier needs real value differences.


**Four or five options**

Three. Not four. Not five.

More than three causes decision paralysis.


**Not knowing why**

If they ask "Why is Better $1,200 more?", you better have a confident answer.

If you can't justify the gap, neither can they.


---

Try Good/Better/Best on your next five quotes. Track the results. Most contractors see 25-35% higher average tickets immediately.

# Part 2: Win the Job

## Chapter 5: Flat Rate vs. Time & Materials

Every contractor faces this choice: do you quote a fixed price, or bill for time and materials?

Both have their place. 

The key is knowing when to use which.


### When to Use Flat Rate

**You've done this job a hundred times**

You know exactly how long it takes, what can go wrong, and what materials you need. Your speed becomes your profit.


**The scope is clear**

Water heater replacement. Panel upgrade. Faucet install. Defined start and end, no mysteries.


**The client wants certainty**

"How much?" is answered with a number, not a range. They can budget, approve, and stop worrying.


**Flat rate advantage**

If you quote $800 and finish in 3 hours instead of 4, you just gave yourself a raise. Your efficiency is rewarded.


### When to Use Time & Materials

**The scope is uncertain**

"My lights keep flickering." "There's water somewhere under the house." You don't know what you'll find until you open it up.


**Old construction**

Pre-1970s homes are surprise factories. What's behind that wall? Nobody knows. Don't lock in a price.


**Scope keeps changing**

"While you're here, can you also..." If the client likes adding things, T&M keeps you protected.


**T&M advantage**

When you find knob-and-tube wiring, active leaks, or a family of raccoons, you're not stuck eating the cost.


### The Hybrid Approach

Best of both worlds: **Quote flat rate for the defined scope. Bill T&M for surprises.**

> "This quote covers replacing your water heater—$1,850 installed. If we find any issues with the gas line or venting that need repair, I'll stop and give you options before proceeding. Any additional work is billed at $145/hour plus materials."

Client gets certainty for the expected work. You're protected for the unexpected.

---

### Presenting T&M Without Scaring Them

T&M makes clients nervous. They imagine a runaway bill with no end.

**Do this**

- Give a realistic range: "Based on what I'm seeing, figure 3-4 hours at $140/hour plus maybe $200 in parts. You're probably looking at $600-800 total."
- Offer a cap: "I'll stop at 4 hours and check in with you before continuing."
- Communicate during: "Good news, we're halfway done and on track."


**Don't do this**

- Start work without discussing rates
- Give no estimate at all
- Surprise them with a bill double what they expected

## Chapter 6: The Psychology of Pricing

The number matters. 

How you present it matters more!

### Anchoring: First Number Wins

Remember anchoring from Chapter 4? The first number someone sees sets their expectations.

Always show your premium option first. When $12,000 is on the page, $8,000 feels like a deal.


### Charm Pricing: Skip It

$4,997 instead of $5,000. Does it work?

For retail, maybe. For contractor services, it looks gimmicky.

You're not a used car lot. Use round numbers. $5,000 or $4,900.


### Confidence: The Closer

Here's the thing: hesitation kills deals.

"Um, so it would probably be around, I don't know, maybe $8,500 or so?" = Client hears: "He's not sure. Maybe I can negotiate."

"This job is $8,500. That includes materials, labor, permit, and a 3-year warranty." = Client hears: "He knows what he's doing."

Same price. Different close rate.


**Confidence comes from:**
- Knowing your numbers (you've done the math)
- Having a system (Good/Better/Best, flat rate book)
- Believing you're worth it (because you are)

## Chapter 7: What Makes a Quote Close

A quote isn't a price. It's a trust document.

The client is looking at your quote thinking: "Can I trust this person to do what they say for what they're charging?"

Your quote either builds that trust or undermines it.


### The Must-Haves

**Professional appearance**

Clean layout, your logo, readable fonts. The client is comparing your quote to two others. The one that looks like a real business wins, even if it costs more.


**Clear scope**

Exactly what you're doing. Specific enough there's no confusion, short enough they'll read it.


**What's included**

Materials (specific: "50-gallon Rheem water heater Model XYZ"), labor, permits, cleanup, warranty.


**What's NOT included**

This is critical. "Does not include: electrical upgrades, drywall repair, painting." Prevents "But I thought that was included" arguments.


**The price**

Clear and prominent. If Good/Better/Best, all three visible.


**Terms**

Payment schedule, change order policy, warranty, expiration date.


**Signature line**

Turns it from a quote into a contract when they sign.


### The Deal-Killers

**Detailed cost breakdowns showing your markup**

Don't show "Materials: $1,000 + 25% = $1,250." Show "Materials: $1,250." They don't need to see your margins.


**Hourly rate on flat-rate quotes**

They'll do the math and decide you're "too expensive per hour" even when the project price is fair.


**Wishy-washy language**

"I think this should cover it." "Prices may change." Either know your price or don't quote.


---

### Terms That Protect You

**Payment:** "50% deposit to start. Balance due on completion."

**Changes:** "Work not in this quote requires a signed change order before proceeding."

**Validity:** "Quote valid for 30 days." Creates urgency, protects you from material price changes.

**Warranty:** "Workmanship guaranteed 3 years. Manufacturer warranties on all equipment."

## Chapter 8: Speed Wins

Here's a stat worth knowing:

**78% of buyers choose the first company that responds.** Not the best. Not the cheapest. The first.

Research from Harvard Business Review found:
- Companies that respond within 5 minutes are 21x more likely to connect
- Within 1 hour: 7x more likely
- After 24 hours: basically dead

Yet the average contractor takes 2-3 days to send a quote.

### The On-Site Quote Advantage

If you can quote before you leave the driveway, do it.

Bring:
- Phone or tablet with quoting app
- Your standard pricing for common jobs
- Ability to generate a PDF on the spot

Leave with a signed contract, not a "I'll think about it."

### Templates Save You

You shouldn't create every quote from scratch.

Build templates for your common jobs:
- Service call (diagnostic + common repairs)
- Equipment replacement (Good/Better/Best pre-built)
- Standard installations

Fill in the specifics, send in minutes.


### The Follow-Up That Works

**Quoted on-site:** Next day text. "Hey, just checking if you had any questions about that panel quote."

**Quoted remotely:** 2-3 days later. "Wanted to make sure you got the quote. Happy to walk through it if that's helpful."

**No response after a week:** One more try. "I know things get busy. If you've decided to go another direction, no worries, just let me know so I can close out the file."

**Don't:**
- Call every day (desperate)
- Lower your price without being asked (weak)
- Bad-mouth competitors (unprofessional)

## Chapter 9: Handling "That's Too Expensive"

No matter how good your pricing, you'll hear objections. Here's how to handle the common ones without caving.

### "That's more than I expected."

**Don't say:** "Well, what were you expecting?" (puts them on defense)

**Do say:** "I get it, these projects add up. Let me walk you through what's included so you can see where the investment goes."

Then: Review scope, emphasize value (warranty, quality, your experience). 

If needed, offer to adjust scope to meet budget.


### "Can you do any better on price?"

**Translation:** "I want to feel like I got a deal."

**Option 1 - Hold firm:** "This is my price for the scope we discussed. I've priced it fairly and I stand behind the work. If budget is a concern, we could look at our options."

**Option 2 - Small concession (not price):** "I can't go lower on price, but I can throw in [extended warranty / free service call / removal of old equipment] if we move forward today."

**Option 3 - Reduce scope:** "I can bring it down if we go with the standard fixtures instead of the upgraded ones. That saves about $600. Would that work?"


### "The other guy was cheaper."

**Don't:** "You get what you pay for" (condescending) or "He's probably unlicensed" (defensive).

**Do:** "I can't speak to their pricing, but here's what's in mine: [warranty, insurance, specific materials, my experience]. Are you comparing the same scope?"

Often cheaper quotes leave things out. 

Help them see it.

**Also valid:** "If their price works for your budget, I understand. If anything comes up later, give me a call. I'm happy to help."

Sometimes the cheap guy burns them. Play the long game.


### "Let me think about it."

**Translation:** Could be anything. Get specific.

**Say:** "Of course, big decision. Is there anything specific you're unsure about, or any info I could provide?"

This opens the door to the real objection. Maybe it's price, timing, spousal approval, or waiting on another quote. 

Now you can address it.

# Part 3: Keep Your Money

## Chapter 10: Change Orders (Where Profits Go to Die)

"While you're here, can you also..."

Those five words have killed more profit margins than any competitor.

### The Problem

**Scope creep** is when the job keeps getting bigger but your price stays the same. A little extra here, a small addition there, and suddenly you've done 20% more work for 0% more money.

### The Fix

Every addition gets priced before you do it.

**The conversation:**

1. "Sure, I can do that."
2. "That would be an additional $350."
3. "Want me to write that up?"
4. Get approval in writing (text or email works)
5. Do the work

**Script:**

> "Hey, just want to make sure we're on the same page. Adding that outlet isn't in the original scope, so it's an extra $150. If that works, I just need a quick OK from you before I start on it."

This isn't awkward. This is professional.

### Why Contractors Don't Do This

You feel like you're nickel-and-diming them. You want to be the "good guy." You figure it'll only take 20 minutes.

**Reality check:** Twenty minutes here, thirty there, a few callbacks. Suddenly you've given away a full day's labor this month. Every month. For years.

That free work adds up, fast.

### Documentation

Doesn't need to be formal. Just written.

Text: "Per our conversation, adding the outlet for $275. Total is now $3,475. OK to proceed?"

Client: "Yes"

Done. Protected.

## Chapter 11: The Software Fee Trap

How much of your revenue goes to software fees?

Most contractors have no idea.

### The Hidden 3%

Many contractor apps include payment processing. Convenient, right?

Check the rates:
- Stripe: 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction
- Square: 2.6-3.3% + per-transaction fees
- Jobber Payments: 2.9% + $0.30
- Housecall Pro: 2.5-2.9%

And here's the catch: **most platforms don't let you use cheaper processors.** They make money on your transaction fees, so you're locked in.

### The Real Cost

| Your Revenue | At 2.9% | What You Lose |
|--------------|---------|---------------|
| $100,000 | $2,900 | A week's pay |
| $300,000 | $8,700 | New tools |
| $500,000 | $14,500 | A helper for 3 months |

That's every year. Forever.

### What to Look For

**Questions to ask about any software**

- Can I use my own payment processor?
- What are the transaction fees?
- Are there per-invoice or per-quote charges?
- What does it cost to export my own data?

Your software should help you make money, not skim off every dollar.

## Chapter 12: Get Paid Faster

You did the work. Now get paid.

### Payment Terms That Work

**Under $2,000:** Due on completion. Maybe net-7 for good clients.

**$2,000-10,000:** 50% deposit to start. 50% on completion.

**Over $10,000:** 30-50% deposit. Progress payments at milestones. 10% on final walkthrough.

### Why Deposits Matter

**Cash flow**

Materials and payroll don't wait.


**Commitment**

Money down = serious client.


**Protection**

If they ghost, you're not fully exposed.


### Invoice Immediately

Send the invoice the second you finish. Not tomorrow. Not "when you get to it." Today.

Why:
- Client is happy (you just finished)
- Number is fresh in their mind
- Momentum is on your side

Every day you wait is a day you wait to get paid.

### Following Up

**Day 1 past due:** "Hey, just checking in. Looks like payment's past due. Any questions?"

**Day 7:** "Following up on that invoice. Let me know if you need it resent."

**Day 14:** "This is now two weeks overdue. Please let me know your plan for payment."

**Day 30+:** Decision time. Small claims, collections, or write it off.

Stay professional. Document everything. Don't let complaints about the work become an excuse not to pay.

### Fire Bad Clients

Some clients aren't worth it. Consider firing them if:
- They consistently pay late
- They dispute every invoice
- They're abusive to you or your crew
- The drama exceeds the revenue

**How:** "I don't think we're the best fit for your needs. I'd recommend [other contractor]. Best of luck."

No explanation needed. Move on.
